**Decision-Making Tiers**:

**Tier 1: Operational Decisions (Day-to-Day)**
- Who decides: Community manager or operations team
- Examples: Event scheduling, space maintenance, vendor selection
- Approval: No community vote required (trust delegation)
- Transparency: Decisions communicated to community after

**Tier 2: Tactical Decisions (Programs & Initiatives)**
- Who decides: Working groups or committees
- Examples: New programs, partnerships, budget allocation for initiatives
- Approval: Working group proposes, community advisory vote
- Transparency: Proposal published 1 week before decision, feedback collected

**Tier 3: Strategic Decisions (Direction & Major Changes)**
- Who decides: All members (democratic vote)
- Examples: Governance changes, major financial commitments, new city expansion
- Approval: 2/3 majority required for approval
- Transparency: Proposal published 2 weeks before vote, town hall discussion, vote recorded

**Voting Mechanisms to Test**:

**1. One-Member-One-Vote** (Simple Democracy)
- Pro: Most democratic and egalitarian
- Con: Majority can oppress minority, no distinction for contribution level
- Test for: Strategic decisions, constitutional amendments

**2. Token-Weighted Voting** (Economic Stake)
- Pro: Aligns incentives with economic commitment
- Con: Can lead to plutocracy (whales control decisions)
- Test for: Financial decisions, investment allocation

**3. Reputation-Based Voting** (Contribution Weighted)
- Pro: Rewards active contributors, not just financial stake
- Con: Reputation measurement can be subjective
- Test for: Community programs, cultural decisions

**4. Quadratic Voting** (Preference Intensity)
- Pro: Allows expressing intensity of preference, reduces domination
- Con: More complex to understand and implement
- Test for: Resource allocation, prioritization decisions

**5. Conviction Voting** (Time-Weighted Preference)
- Pro: Rewards long-term commitment, prevents manipulation
- Con: Slow decision-making, requires continuous participation
- Test for: Funding decisions, long-term strategic choices

**Recommendation**: Start with **One-Member-One-Vote** for simplicity, experiment with others as community matures

**Governance Bodies**:

**1. General Assembly** (All Members)
- Meets: Monthly
- Purpose: Strategic decisions, community updates, transparent communication
- Format: Town hall style, open discussion, formal votes when needed

**2. Council** (Elected Representatives)
- Size: 5-9 members
- Elected: Every 6 months by community
- Purpose: Tactical decisions, oversight, conflict resolution
- Meets: Weekly or bi-weekly

**3. Working Groups** (Thematic Focus)
- Examples: Events, Communications, Partnerships, Finance, Governance
- Open membership (anyone can join and contribute)
- Purpose: Execute initiatives, propose ideas, distribute leadership

**4. Stewards** (Day-to-Day Operations)
- Hired or volunteer roles
- Examples: Community manager, space manager, events coordinator
- Purpose: Operations, member support, program execution
- Accountable to: Council and General Assembly

**Conflict Resolution Process**:

**Level 1: Peer-to-Peer**
- Encourage members to resolve conflicts directly
- Provide communication training and frameworks (non-violent communication, etc.)

**Level 2: Mediation**
- Trained mediators (community members or external) facilitate resolution
- Voluntary participation, confidential process
- Goal: Mutually agreeable solution

**Level 3: Council Arbitration**
- Council reviews case and makes binding decision
- Used when mediation fails or conflict affects broader community
- Decision can be appealed to General Assembly

**Level 4: Exit**
- If resolution impossible, member can exit with refund
- Or community can vote to remove member (requires 2/3 majority)
- Protects both individual and collective
